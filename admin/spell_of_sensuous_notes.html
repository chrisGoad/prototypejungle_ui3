Reactions to "The Spell of the Sensuous" by David Abram
The first chapter is interesting, and his own experiences are described vividly. On the conceptual side, it describes a tradition of western thought, coming from Christianity and the enlightenment, which posits that humans are the only earthly repository of spirit, and that the natural world outside the human is a vast soulless mechanism. This tradition of thought justifies the exploitation by humans of non-human nature. This tradition is not only incorrect in its judgements, but, as we are now seeing, is disastrous for our own prospects as humans. Also, sensitivity and appreciation of non-human nature is crucial to a good life. In these matters I am entirely in sympathy with Abram.
I read the next chapter (X) and skimmed the rest of the book. The chapter contains two parts, one on Husserl and one on Merleau-Ponty. I had reactions to each part, which I will detail here.
In part 1 Abram retold one aspect of Husserl's work. This aspect I would call "phenomena without phenomenology". What I mean by this is that Abram asked us to take phenomena (experience) seriously and not to identify the world with the world as scientificly understood, but did not say anything about phenomenology - the study of phenomena. The doctrine of the primacy of phenomena is indeed a core part of Husserl's message: the view that we encounter our experience of things first, and much later learn of or construct scientific theories. The latter are very different from the former. An encounter with a bird, rock, or the sky as we experience them are not encounters with atoms or quantum fields! The scientific picture of the world is in this sense embedded in the world of phenomena. Some people think that the embedding goes both ways: that the phenomenal world is embedded in the world as scientifically understood.
The other major part of Husserl's work was phenomenogy. For this purpose, Husserl introduced such terms as Epoche, Hyle, Horizon, Noema, and Noesis. Abram does not touch on phenomenology, as opposed to the primacy of phenomena. Nonetheless he manages to spend dozens of pages on the matter. A paragraph or two would have sufficed, it seems to me.
In part 2, Abram turns to MP. I know Husserl's work much better than that of Merleau-Ponty, and was not entirely able to disentangle which views should be attributed to Abram and which to MP.
The major themes of the part seemed to me to be 1) The activites of the mind are carried out in a physical body, and this bodily aspect deserves attention. 2) Perception is participatory: subjects of perception are not passive; perception is always an interaction between active perceiver and active percievded.
The first point I agree with. But although thought, perception, and so on arise in a body, and are affected by bodily events and properties, they themselves are not bodies or parts of bodies. Saying that everything arises in a body does not solve any problems. One cannot explain a single activity of body or mind just by saying that it is carried out in a physical body.
The "all is embodied" mantra leads to long poetic statements in tsos, statements which do not always provide illumination
2) is only true when the thing experienced is either biological, or something that is otherwise changed by us, such as a chair, or a machine that responds to us. It is not true of the trees or rocks beside the road which we fail to crash into, but which we nonetheless perceive, or the distant mountains, or the sky. Also it is dubious that a chair experiences, in any sense, us sitting in it. Of course we may imagine that things respond to us when they don't. We might pray to a stone idol, and imagine that it responds. So the reciprocity of perception is part of our phenomenal world, but is sometimes illusory.
An his striving for a poetic take on the sensuous world Abram sometimes goes too far. Example from chapter 6 part 1: "The precise contour of the horizon varies considerably in different terrains, yet whenever we climb to a prominent vantage point, the circular character of the visible world becomes explicit. Thus cyclical time, the experiential time of an oral culture, has the same shape as perceivable space. And the two circles are, in truth, one." In truth one? This is equivalent to saying, "time=space=jelly doughnut". I doubt that this nonsensical thought has ever entered an indiginous mind.
